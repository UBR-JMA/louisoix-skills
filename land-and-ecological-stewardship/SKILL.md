# Land and Ecological Stewardship

Land is never neutral. The ground beneath a community's feet has a history — of Indigenous presence, displacement, colonization, labor, and claim. It exists within a present — of tenure arrangements, debt, ecological condition, and contested use. It opens toward a future that is either being actively shaped or passively allowed to happen.

Communities with a significant relationship to land — whether farming, foraging, maintaining gardens, stewarding woodlands, tending animals, or simply inhabiting a specific place with intention — face a distinct set of governance questions, labor tensions, and ethical obligations that general community frameworks often underaddress. This skill is for those communities.

Use this skill when:
- Land tenure is uncertain, contested, or subject to external pressure (rent, debt, ownership dispute)
- The community occupies or uses land with Indigenous history and needs to think honestly about what that means
- Food production is a significant community activity and the labor of growing, harvesting, cooking, and preserving is creating tension or needs explicit governance
- Ecological decisions — how to treat soil, water, animals, pests, forests — are becoming governance questions
- Animals are part of community life and the community is navigating the full arc of their care: birth, labor, death, grief
- The community's relationship to its specific place is emotionally and spiritually significant, or is threatened
- Seasonal rhythms are shaping or being suppressed by community life
- Environmental justice — whose land is polluted, who bears environmental burdens — is relevant to the community's situation
- Land is becoming a source of conflict: competing visions, labor resentments, debt pressure, philosophical divisions

## Land Tenure as Foundational Material Condition

Land tenure is not a legal abstraction. It is the most foundational material condition a land-based community faces. The difference between owning land, renting it, and having informal access rights is the difference between stability and precarity at the community's root.

### What tenure arrangements actually mean

**Ownership** provides security: the community can make long-term investments in soil health, perennial crops, infrastructure, and ecological relationships. It can experiment, fail, and try again. It can pass the land to the next generation of the community. Ownership does not mean freedom from financial pressure — land taxes, mortgages, and maintenance costs are real — but it means the community is not subject to a landlord's decision to sell, raise rent, or repurpose the property.

**Renting** introduces fundamental instability. A community can invest years of labor into building soil health and then lose the land — and everything built into it — to a lease termination. Perennial crops, orchards, food forests, and regenerative soil systems require decade-scale commitments; renting makes that investment precarious. Renting also means the community's physical home exists at someone else's discretion. This shapes planning horizons, willingness to invest, and members' sense of security in profound ways. It does not make land-based community impossible, but it changes the calculations.

**Use rights and informal access** (agreements with neighboring landowners, community land trusts, easements) occupy a middle ground. They can provide relative stability without the capital requirements of ownership, but the security depends entirely on the nature and enforceability of the arrangement. A handshake agreement with a friendly neighbor is fragile; a conservation easement with legal backing is much less so. Understand exactly what you have before planning around it.

**Collective or trust ownership** structures — land trusts, cooperative ownership, commons arrangements — deserve serious attention from communities committed to long-term stewardship. These structures can remove land from the speculative real estate market, make it permanently affordable and accessible, and tie its use to community values rather than market value. The CLT (Community Land Trust) model has decades of practice behind it; explore it if ownership is a goal but capital is limited.

### Acquiring land tenure

When a community is seeking to move from renting to owning, or from informal access to formal security, the process is almost always slower and harder than anticipated. Key considerations:

**Capital**: Land acquisition requires either significant collective savings, external financing, or grants (some exist for agricultural land, conservation, or affordable housing). Be honest about what the community has and needs.

**Legal structure**: The community needs a legal entity capable of holding property — a nonprofit, LLC, cooperative, or trust. This has governance implications; the entity that holds title has real power over the land, so it should reflect the community's governance values.

**Zoning and agricultural use**: Not all land is zoned for residential use, agricultural use, or both. Understand the zoning before acquiring land, and investigate whether variance or rezoning is achievable.

**Agricultural land debt is heavy**: A community that takes on a mortgage to acquire land is also taking on a significant ongoing financial obligation. The labor required to service that debt — either through farm income or through members' external income — is real, and should be planned for honestly rather than optimistically. Land debt has ended many communities.

**Incremental paths**: Sometimes a community can acquire partial security before full ownership — a long-term lease (10, 20, 99 years), a right of first refusal if the property is sold, or a formal use agreement with a landlord willing to provide stability. These don't substitute for ownership but can buy time and reduce precarity.

## Indigenous Land and Land-Back

Every community occupying land in the Americas — and most of the rest of the world — occupies land with Indigenous history. In the United States and Canada, that occupation is recent, legally contested in many cases, and the product of processes of dispossession, forced removal, and genocide that are not distant history but ongoing material reality.

This is not optional context. It is foundational. The question of how a community relates to the Indigenous history and present of its land is not a question for a diversity committee or an acknowledgment at the beginning of an event. It is a question that belongs at the center of how a land-based community understands itself.

### Whose land

Start with the specific. Use resources like Native Land Digital (native-land.ca) to identify which Indigenous nations and peoples have historical and ongoing relationships with the specific land your community occupies. This is not always a single answer — land may have been home to multiple nations over time, may be subject to treaty claims, or may be actively claimed by contemporary Indigenous peoples. Learn the specifics, not just a general awareness.

If there are active Indigenous nations or communities near the land your community occupies, they exist. They have governance structures, representatives, and relationships. The question is not whether to acknowledge them but whether to actually engage with them.

### Land acknowledgment as starting point, not endpoint

Land acknowledgments — the practice of naming whose land you occupy at the beginning of gatherings — have become standard practice in progressive communities. They are not without value. They make visible what is often invisible: the Indigenous history of specific places. They are a starting point.

They become hollow — worse than hollow, they become a form of absolution — when they substitute for material engagement. When a community recites whose land it occupies and then proceeds as if that knowledge doesn't obligate anything, the acknowledgment functions as a kind of ritual cleansing: we named it, so now we don't have to do anything about it.

Acknowledgment that means something is acknowledgment that leads somewhere. Where does it lead?

### Meaningful relationships and material obligations

Land-back is both a political demand and a spectrum of practices. At one end is literal land return — title transfer of land to Indigenous nations. At the other are ongoing material relationships that don't involve return of title but do involve real obligations and real benefits flowing to Indigenous communities.

**Land taxes and voluntary rent**: Some communities make ongoing payments — conceptualized as rent or tax — to the Indigenous nations on whose land they live. This is not a legal obligation in most cases (though some treaties do create legal obligations worth understanding). It is a material relationship of ongoing acknowledgment and redistribution. Organizations like Honor the Earth and Shuumi Land Tax (for the Bay Area) have created structures that communities can participate in. If such a structure doesn't exist in your region, some communities create their own arrangements through direct relationship with Indigenous nations.

**Ask before assuming**: The most important principle in building relationships with Indigenous communities is to ask what they actually want and need rather than deciding for them. Some Indigenous nations want direct relationships with intentional communities occupying their traditional territories. Some don't. Some want financial support for specific programs. Some want labor. Some want to be left alone. The relationship should be driven by what they want, not by what makes your community feel better about its situation.

**How to initiate contact with Indigenous governance**: Many communities want to build relationships with local Indigenous nations but don't know how to start. The practical steps:

First, identify the specific nations whose territory you occupy. Native Land Digital (native-land.ca) is the starting point, but it is a reference tool, not a definitive authority. Cross-reference with your state's tribal consultation lists, historical records, and (if you are near a reservation) the nation's own published territorial claims.

Second, look for the nation's official governance structure. Most federally recognized tribes in the US have tribal council offices, tribal administrator contacts, and (increasingly) websites. Reach out to the official governance structure — not to an individual Indigenous person you happen to know socially, and not to an Indigenous organization that does not have a specific relationship with that nation's governance. Working around official governance because it seems more efficient is a form of disrespect.

Third, write a letter or email that is honest, specific, and asks a genuine question. Tell them who your community is, where you are located, and what you are hoping to explore. The question should be open: "We occupy land within your traditional territory and want to understand what, if any, relationship you are interested in having with us. We are open to learning what that might look like." This is different from arriving with a proposal about what you want to offer.

Fourth, be prepared for silence, slowness, or no. Many nations receive many requests and have limited administrative capacity. Some have decided not to engage with settler communities for reasons of self-protection and bandwidth. No response is an answer, and it should be respected, not interpreted as an invitation to try harder.

If the nation does respond and is interested in relationship, follow their lead about what form that takes, what the pace should be, and who should be involved. Building genuine relationship takes years, not a meeting.

**Ecological knowledge and land stewardship**: Many Indigenous communities have deep ecological knowledge of the specific land your community occupies — which plants are native, which are invasive, how the land has been managed historically, what its water systems are. Building relationships that allow this knowledge to inform your stewardship practices (with proper credit, compensation, and relationship, not extractive appropriation) serves both ecological health and right relationship.

**Political solidarity**: Indigenous communities are often in active political struggle — over water rights, mineral rights, treaty rights, land return. Supporting those struggles materially (donations, presence, organizing) is part of what a meaningful relationship looks like.

### How to think about this honestly

The honest version of this conversation is not comfortable. Your community is likely occupying land taken by force within the last few centuries from people whose descendants are still here and still have relationships to it. The legal structures that make your ownership or use possible are built on that dispossession. This is not something that can be fully resolved through any individual community practice. It is part of the political economy of land in the Americas.

What this means practically: your community should not expect to arrive at a conclusion where it has done enough and can stop thinking about it. The question of right relationship to Indigenous land is ongoing, not checkable. The goal is not absolution but genuine, material, sustained engagement — building actual relationships, meeting actual obligations, supporting actual political struggles — rather than the comfort of having named the problem.

This does not mean paralysis. You are on the land now. The work is to be in relationship with its history and its Indigenous present in ways that are honest, material, and ongoing.

## Food Production as Community Labor

For communities where growing, harvesting, processing, preserving, and cooking food are significant activities, food production is a major site of community labor — and therefore a major site of potential inequity, conflict, and governance challenge.

### The full scope of food labor

Food production encompasses far more than farming:

- Soil preparation, planting, transplanting, thinning
- Ongoing maintenance: weeding, watering, pest management, trellising
- Harvesting: physical work, timing, coordination
- Post-harvest handling: washing, sorting, curing, storing
- Processing: canning, fermenting, drying, freezing, milling
- Cooking and meal preparation: daily or collective meals
- Cleanup and kitchen maintenance
- Planning: what to grow, when to plant, crop rotation decisions
- Seed saving and propagation
- Equipment maintenance and repair
- Animal integration: composting, manure management, pest control

When communities talk about "farming together," they often mean the visible, pleasant parts of food production — planting seeds in spring, harvesting in fall — and undercount the ongoing labor of everything in between. This sets up resentment and imbalance.

### Who does this work

Apply the same analysis here that community-material-analysis applies to domestic labor generally: map who actually does the food production work before assuming it is distributed equitably.

Common patterns in land-based communities:

- Women and femme members disproportionately do kitchen work (processing, cooking, cleanup) while men disproportionately do visible field work and equipment management
- The person with the most agricultural knowledge becomes an informal expert whose labor is drawn on constantly without formal recognition
- Some members engage enthusiastically during planting and harvest (the photogenic moments) while others carry the maintenance labor between those peaks
- Members who live closest to the production areas end up doing more by proximity
- People with physical limitations or disabilities are implicitly excluded from food labor in ways that are never named

These patterns should be made visible through the same kind of labor audit that community-material-analysis recommends for domestic labor. Food production labor is community labor; it should be tracked, recognized, and distributed deliberately.

### Decision-making about food production

Communities need explicit governance for food decisions:

**What to grow and how much**: This has implications for labor, diet, and ecological priorities. Someone has to make these calls; that someone should be accountable to the community rather than simply the person who is most knowledgeable or most invested.

**Agricultural philosophy**: Does the community prioritize yield, biodiversity, soil health, heritage varieties, self-sufficiency, or surplus for sale? These can be in tension. When the person who knows the most about farming makes unilateral decisions about these tradeoffs, governance has effectively been delegated to expertise — which may be appropriate, or may not be.

**Food security vs. other values**: How much of the community's food should come from its own production? What is the role of purchased food? These decisions have labor implications: higher food self-sufficiency requires more labor, and that labor has to come from somewhere.

**Who benefits**: If the community sells surplus or otherwise generates income from food production, who benefits and how? This is a class and equity question as much as an agricultural one.

### Connection to community-material-analysis

When food production labor is a source of persistent tension — some people working too hard, others not enough; some members excluded by physical capacity; cooking labor falling along gender lines — community-material-analysis provides the structural framework for diagnosing and addressing it. The food production context does not change the analysis; it gives it a specific domain to work in. Use both skills together when food labor is the recurring tension.

## Ecological Stewardship as Governance

How a community treats its land — the soil, water, plant communities, pest populations, and broader ecological relationships — is a governance question, not just a technical one. Ecological decisions express values, create obligations, and generate conflict when those values diverge.

### Soil health as community commitment

Soil is not a substrate; it is a living community. Topsoil takes centuries to build and can be lost in a season of poor management. A community that farms its land extractively — depleting organic matter, neglecting perennials, running off topsoil, relying on synthetic inputs to compensate for degraded soil ecology — is making a choice about its relationship to the land it occupies. A community that builds soil actively — composting, cover cropping, integrating animals, reducing tillage, planting perennials — is making a different choice.

These choices have a temporal dimension that is worth naming explicitly: soil health investments don't pay off in months, they pay off in decades. Communities that plan only to the end of their current lease, or whose members expect to move on in a few years, may rationally under-invest in soil. Communities that are planning for permanence have reason to invest deeply. The governance question is: what time horizon is the community actually planning around, and do the community's land management practices reflect that?

### Water

Water management is both a practical and an ethical dimension of land stewardship. Key questions:

- What are the community's water sources (well, municipal, rain catchment, stream), and how secure are they?
- How does the community's land use affect water quality — for itself and for downstream neighbors?
- What relationships does the community have to the watershed it inhabits?
- During drought, how does the community prioritize water use? What crops or practices are negotiable, and which are not?

Water is a shared resource at a scale beyond the community's land boundary. Thinking about it only within those boundaries misses the ecological and political reality. Watershed thinking — understanding the community's land as part of a larger hydrological system with obligations to other users and to ecological health — is part of honest stewardship.

### Biodiversity, native plants, and invasive species

Communities vary widely in their commitment to biodiversity and native plant restoration. This is a values question as much as an ecological one. A community that wants to maximize food production may see a meadow as underutilized. A community that prioritizes ecological relationship may see that meadow as exactly the right use of that land.

When these values coexist in the same community — and they frequently do — the conflict that results is often really about competing visions of what the land is for. Making those visions explicit, and creating a governance process for working through them, is more productive than relitigating specific decisions (this meadow, that area of brambles) without naming the underlying disagreement.

### Pest management and philosophical conflict

Pest management — how to handle insects, rodents, deer, fungi, and other organisms that affect the community's crops — is a site of genuine philosophical conflict in many communities.

Some members will hold a position of minimal intervention: let the ecosystem find its balance, accept losses as part of ecological relationship. Others will hold a position of active defense: the community has invested labor and resources in this food production, and protecting that investment is appropriate. Others will hold animal-welfare-based positions: no rodenticide, no lethal control of larger animals regardless of damage.

These positions are not easily reconciled. They reflect real differences in ecological philosophy, relationship to other species, and values about the purpose of the land. Communities are better served by naming these as genuine disagreements requiring deliberate governance than by assuming they can be resolved through compromise or expertise.

### Regenerative vs. extractive relationships

The question of whether the community's relationship to its land is regenerative or extractive is worth asking periodically and honestly. Regenerative relationships build soil, support biodiversity, sequester carbon, improve water quality, and leave the land more ecologically vital than it was found. Extractive relationships deplete soil, reduce biodiversity, increase erosion, and degrade the land's long-term capacity.

Most communities fall somewhere in between, and movement along this spectrum is possible. What matters is whether the community is being honest about where it actually is, and whether it is committed to moving in a regenerative direction over time — not as aspiration only, but as practice with accountability.

### Rewilding and Indigenous ecological knowledge

Rewilding — the practice of restoring ecological processes, removing invasive species, reintroducing native plants and (where appropriate) animals, and reducing human management in favor of ecological self-regulation — has gained significant attention in land stewardship circles. It is valuable, and it has a relationship to Indigenous land management that communities should understand rather than ignore.

Most land in the Americas was not pre-contact wilderness. It was actively managed by Indigenous peoples over millennia: through controlled burning, tending, selective harvesting, and cultivation practices that shaped the plant communities, forest structures, and animal populations that European colonizers mistook for untouched nature. What rewilding often aims to restore is, in part, a landscape that was the product of Indigenous land management — not an absence of human stewardship.

This has direct implications for communities that want to pursue rewilding:

- **Native plant communities are not simply "natural"**; they often reflect historical Indigenous management. The species composition, the fire ecology, the water retention of healthy native landscapes was actively produced. Rewilding that ignores this history may be trying to restore something it doesn't fully understand.

- **Indigenous knowledge holders are relevant experts** — not on romanticized pre-contact conditions, but on the actual history and ecology of specific places as maintained through living practice and oral tradition. Accessing this knowledge requires genuine relationship, not appropriation of published summaries.

- **Some rewilding practices and some Indigenous management practices are in alignment**; others are in tension. Controlled burning is one where many Indigenous nations and rewilding practitioners agree on the principle but may differ on specifics. Be specific about what the community is actually trying to do rather than assuming alignment.

The synthesis here is not that rewilding is wrong or that Indigenous ecological knowledge is sacred and inaccessible. It is that communities serious about ecological stewardship would do well to understand these histories and build relationships that allow them to draw on them — through the same process of genuine relationship described in the Indigenous land section above, not through extractive consultation.

## Animals in Community

When animals are part of community life — livestock, working animals, companion animals — they bring dimensions of relationship, labor, ethics, and grief that communities rarely plan for adequately.

### Livestock: ethics, labor, and the full lifecycle

For communities that raise livestock for food (chickens, pigs, goats, cattle, rabbits), the ethical questions are real and should be worked through collectively rather than assumed.

**What the community actually believes about killing animals for food** is not a question with a universal answer. Communities contain vegetarians and omnivores, people who have killed animals and people who never have. Assuming these differences are irrelevant, or that the farming-inclined members will simply handle it, is a governance failure. The community needs to know what it believes before the animals arrive, not after.

**The labor of animal care** is daily, non-negotiable, and binding. Animals require feeding and watering every day — during illness, bad weather, personal crisis, travel. This is different from the labor of field crops, which can be neglected for a few days without catastrophe. The community needs to plan explicitly for who carries this labor, how it rotates, who covers when, and what happens when the primary caretaker is unavailable. Burn out among animal caretakers is common and serious.

**Slaughter as community labor**: When a community raises animals for food, someone has to kill them. This can be done by a hired butcher, by a few community members with skill and willingness, or by the community collectively. Each choice has implications — for cost, skill development, relationship to the animals, and who bears the emotional labor of killing. Communities that quietly delegate this to whoever is willing, without ever making it an explicit governance decision, often find it becomes a source of resentment or unacknowledged burden.

**Humane treatment as community standard**: What does the community believe animals deserve while they are alive and at death? What are the minimum standards the community will hold itself to even when it is inconvenient or expensive? Articulating these standards before acquiring animals creates accountability that is much harder to establish afterward.

### Working animals

Dogs, horses, donkeys, and other working animals occupy a particular place: they are not primarily food animals, they have long-term relationships with community members, and their care is both labor and relationship. Questions that need governance:

- Who makes decisions about the working animal's care, training, and welfare?
- Who is responsible for the animal's costs?
- What happens if the animal's primary human leaves the community?
- If the animal is dangerous or causes harm, who decides what happens?

### Companion animals

Companion animals in community settings generate predictable conflicts: allergies, fear, territorial disputes between animals, differing standards of care, animals that are beloved by some and a burden to others. Communities that allow companion animals should have explicit agreements about:

- Where animals can be and where they cannot
- Who is responsible for an animal's behavior and any harm it causes
- What happens if the animal and the community's living situation are incompatible

### The death of community animals

When a community animal dies — whether slaughtered, euthanized, or naturally — the grief that results is real and often under-acknowledged. Animals are not peripheral to community life for the people who care for them. Their deaths can be genuinely painful.

This is especially complex when the death is one the community chose (slaughter, euthanasia). The person who was most attached to the animal may need space to grieve a death that others in the community regard as normal or necessary. Creating space for this grief — naming it, allowing it, not rushing past it — is part of the dignity the community extends to the humans in it, even when the death was of an animal.

The community should think in advance about how it will mark animal deaths. A brief acknowledgment, a meal shared from the animal's body with gratitude named, a moment of grief — these practices honor both the animal's life and the humans who cared for it.

## Place Attachment and Place Loss

Communities with deep relationships to specific land develop attachments to that place — its light, its sounds, its soil smell, its specific trees and waterways and seasonal rhythms — that are not just practical but embodied, emotional, and for many people spiritual. This attachment is real and should be named as real, not treated as sentiment that needs to be kept in check.

### What place attachment is

Place attachment is the bond that forms between people and specific geographical locations through sustained experience and relationship. It is not generic appreciation for nature; it is particular relationship to this soil, this hillside, this creek, this view in winter morning light. Communities that have lived on the same land for years develop this attachment inevitably.

Place attachment is also often unevenly distributed within a community. Some members have been on the land for decades; others arrived last year. Some have deep emotional investments in specific places on the land; others relate to it more practically. When the community faces a decision about its land — whether to change it, leave it, or defend it — these differences in attachment surface and can be sources of significant conflict.

### Place loss and land grief

If a community loses access to land it has inhabited — through lease termination, eviction, foreclosure, or forced sale — the grief that results is real and often underestimated by people who have not experienced it. This is not metaphorical grief. The loss of a land one has labored on and loved, the loss of the specific light of a particular hillside, the end of relationships with specific trees and soil and water — this is genuine loss.

Communities facing potential land loss should treat it as they would any other major anticipated grief: with honesty, with preparation, with space for the range of feelings that members will have, and without requiring people to be stronger or more practical than they actually are.

**When land loss is coming**: Name it clearly and early. Communities that maintain denial about a coming land loss until the last moment deprive members of time to grieve, plan, and adjust. The community deserves the truth in time to respond to it.

**Gathering and documenting**: Before leaving land the community has known deeply, there is value in deliberately gathering what can be gathered — seeds from plants cultivated over years, documentation of what was built and learned, photographs, maps, stories. This is not the same as keeping the land, but it carries something forward.

**The next place**: If the community can reconstitute itself in a new place, it will take time to develop the same depth of relationship. The new place is not a substitute for the lost place; it is a different relationship that will develop in its own time. Expecting the new place to feel like home immediately, or pathologizing the grief that the new place isn't home yet, misses the nature of place attachment.

### Displacement and environmental injustice

Land loss is not random. Gentrification displaces communities of color. Industrial agriculture forces out small farmers. Eminent domain and infrastructure development disproportionately take land from poor and working-class communities. The political economy of land dispossession operates along familiar fault lines of race, class, and power.

For communities experiencing or facing displacement, the grief is real and the politics are real simultaneously. Both can be held: this is painful loss, and this is an injustice. The community can organize politically to fight for its land while also allowing the grief of potential loss. These are not contradictory.

## Seasonal Rhythms

Land shapes time. Agricultural communities experience time differently than urban industrial ones: the year is not a flat progression of interchangeable weeks but a cycle of planting, growing, harvest, dormancy, and return. These rhythms are not incidental to farming; they are foundational to how land-based life works.

### Honoring seasonal rhythms in community life

Communities that allow seasonal rhythms to shape their governance and planning — rather than imposing a consistent year-round schedule on all activities — tend to live more harmoniously with the land they inhabit.

**Spring** is the time of intensive preparation: soil work, planting, starting, building. Energy is high and the labor demands are high. Community planning and governance that pulls heavily on members' time during spring planting creates conflict with the land's demands.

**Summer** is the time of maintenance and growth: weeding, watering, tending, watching. The labor is sustained rather than intensive. Summer can also be a time of some community expansion — events, visitors, apprentices.

**Fall** is harvest time: intensive labor with high reward, celebration possible, the satisfaction of abundance. Harvest is also time pressure; what needs to come in before frost has its own deadline that doesn't negotiate with the community's governance calendar.

**Winter** is the time of rest, planning, and repair. The land sleeps. This is the time for deep community conversations, governance review, long-term planning, and personal renewal. Communities that don't give winter its proper weight — that treat it as just slower summer — miss the regenerative function that fallow time provides for people as much as for soil.

### The tension with industrial time

Modern economic life does not honor seasonal rhythms. Jobs don't offer extra leave during planting and harvest. School schedules don't adjust for the agricultural calendar. Social expectations of year-round consistent availability are built around industrial, not agricultural, time.

Communities that try to operate both in the agricultural rhythm and the industrial schedule without acknowledging the tension will find members burning out during the intensive seasons — too many obligations outside the land competing with the land's demands. Naming this tension explicitly — and making deliberate choices about which obligations are negotiable and which are not — is part of honest governance in a land-based community.

### Seasonal celebrations and marking

The agricultural calendar provides natural occasions for community celebration and marking: first planting, midsummer, first harvest, last harvest, the dark of winter, the return of light. These celebrations are not decoration; they are how communities orient themselves in time, express gratitude for the land's gifts, grieve what didn't grow, and renew their commitment to the work.

Communities benefit from developing seasonal rituals that are their own — drawn from members' traditions, from Indigenous practices of the land (with appropriate relationship and permission, not appropriation), from ecological observation, and from their own experience of the land. These rituals accumulate meaning over years and become part of what the community is.

## Environmental Justice

Environmental justice is not separate from land stewardship; it is part of the political context within which land is accessed, used, and degraded.

### Who bears environmental burdens

The distribution of environmental harm in the United States and globally is not random. Industrial polluters — manufacturing plants, waste facilities, factory farms, chemical plants, highways — are disproportionately sited near communities of color and poor communities. The land that is most available and affordable for intentional communities is often land that has been degraded by industrial use, or land in areas burdened by existing pollution. These are not coincidences; they are products of zoning history, political power, and systematic devaluation of low-income and communities of color.

A community acquiring land should understand the environmental history of that land and its surrounding area. Is there industrial contamination? What is the air quality? What are the water quality conditions? Who were the prior uses? Acquiring land that is or may be contaminated creates both legal and health risks; it also places the community in relationship to an environmental justice history it should understand.

### Environmental racism and land access

The history of who has been able to own and hold land in the United States is inseparable from race. The Homestead Act gave land to white settlers on Indigenous land. Black communities were systematically denied access to the programs that built white family wealth through land. Redlining confined Black families to neighborhoods that were then systematically under-resourced and often sited near industrial hazards. Agricultural land held by Black farmers was stolen through fraud, legal manipulation, and violence throughout the 20th century.

Land-based intentional communities that are predominantly white exist within this history whether or not they acknowledge it. When communities wonder why they have difficulty attracting or retaining members of color, one honest answer is that the relationship of land ownership to race in this country makes land-based white community a fraught and historically loaded invitation. Understanding this — rather than being baffled by it — is part of the work.

### The community's own environmental footprint

A community committed to ecological stewardship should be honest about its own environmental impact: its waste streams, its energy use, its water use, its transportation patterns, the supply chains it depends on for inputs it doesn't produce itself. Ecological commitment that focuses only on the beauty of what is grown while ignoring these broader impacts is partial and self-flattering. The goal is not perfection — it is honest accounting and ongoing improvement.

## When Land Becomes a Source of Conflict

Land generates specific conflicts that communities are often not prepared for.

### Competing visions for land use

When community members hold genuinely different visions for what the land should be — one person sees it as a maximally productive farm, another as a wildlife sanctuary, another as a community gathering space — the conflicts that result are not resolvable through communication alone. They require governance processes that can make genuine collective decisions about competing values.

Communities often avoid these decisions for years by allowing the status quo to persist or by letting the most invested person make decisions by default. This deferred governance tends to produce eventual explosion rather than resolution. Better to have the conversation about what the land is for, and make a genuine collective decision, even if that decision is painful for some members.

### Labor distribution in farming

The gap between people who do most of the farming labor and people who enjoy the fruits (literal and figurative) of that labor without contributing proportionately is one of the most common and corrosive tensions in land-based communities. The person working dawn to dusk in the garden while others attend to their own projects develops resentment; the others often don't see the imbalance because they're not in the garden.

Apply the community-material-analysis labor audit specifically to food production work. Map who does what, when, how much. Make the map visible to the community. Then make deliberate decisions about how to redistribute labor or acknowledge and compensate the imbalance — not wait for the overworked person to finally burn out or leave.

### Ecological philosophy differences

Communities where members hold different ecological philosophies — about chemical use, pest management, GMOs, animal welfare, acceptable tradeoffs between productivity and ecology — should work these differences through in advance of acquiring land and animals, and revisit them when disagreements emerge. These are genuine value differences, not misunderstandings, and they need genuine governance rather than the assumption that the most knowledgeable farmer will decide.

### When a faction is blocking Indigenous engagement

Some communities contain members who actively resist engagement with Indigenous land and governance — who dismiss land acknowledgment as performative, contest whether the community "really" occupies Indigenous land, argue that past dispossession is not their responsibility, or refuse to participate in any material relationship with nearby nations.

This is a community conflict with a specific shape. The people resisting engagement are not simply uninformed; often they have ideological commitments (property rights, national narrative, theological frameworks) or emotional responses (guilt that becomes denial) that make engagement feel threatening. Treating this as a knowledge gap that can be closed with better information usually doesn't work.

What can be done:

Name the conflict as a values conflict, not a knowledge deficit. The question is not "do you know enough to understand?" but "what does this community believe it owes to the people whose land we occupy?" That is a community values question that deserves a governance process, not just individual education.

Bring the question of Indigenous engagement to explicit community decision-making. The goal is not to require everyone to share the same feeling about it, but to reach a community-level decision about what the community will do. A member who personally thinks land acknowledgment is hollow can nonetheless agree that the community will make an annual donation to the local nation's language revitalization program. Separate the emotional and ideological arguments from the practical decisions.

Hold the blocking without pathologizing it, but don't let it prevent the community from acting. A faction that disagrees with Indigenous engagement as a matter of principle is not entitled to veto the community's relationship to the land it occupies. If the community as a whole has decided to build a relationship, dissenters can dissent on record while the community moves forward.

### The weight of land debt

When a community carries debt to hold its land — mortgage, land contract, private loan — that debt exerts constant pressure on every other decision. The need to make loan payments can override ecological priorities (need to maximize yield), community capacity (can't invest in long-term soil building), and member wellbeing (everyone must contribute financially to service the debt). Land debt is not an unfortunate background condition; it is a structural force that shapes the community's behavior.

Communities carrying land debt should have explicit, regular conversations about the debt's effects on their decisions and their wellbeing. They should know what the community would need to do to service the debt if member income dropped. They should have a plan for what happens if a large unexpected expense arises. And they should regularly ask whether the debt is bearable, or whether carrying it is costing the community more than the land is worth.

## Coordination

This skill works within the Louisoix community care suite. It is most useful in conjunction with:

**community-material-analysis** — when food production labor is a recurring source of tension, or when ecological stewardship decisions have embedded labor equity questions. Use community-material-analysis to map who does the farm labor, how it falls along race and gender lines, and what structural interventions might address the imbalance. Land-and-ecological-stewardship provides the domain context; community-material-analysis provides the analytical framework.

**cultural-competency** — for the Indigenous land and land-back work, and for environmental racism. Cultural-competency provides the framework for navigating power and historical harm; land-and-ecological-stewardship provides the specific land context. These skills are deeply intertwined here: the question of whose land the community occupies is both a land question and a cultural and political one.

**economic-precarity** — when land debt, food insecurity, or the financial precarity of farming are the primary concerns. Land debt is a form of economic precarity at community scale, and it should be analyzed with the same honesty and structural clarity that economic-precarity applies to individual and household precarity.

**organizational-stewardship** — when ecological decisions, animal care decisions, or land use conflicts need formal governance processes. The decision-making questions raised in this skill — what to grow, how to manage pests, what to do with land the community might lose — are governance questions that organizational-stewardship can help design processes for.

Invoke land-and-ecological-stewardship directly when the primary presenting question is about the community's relationship to its land, food production, animals, or place. Invoke through Louisoix when land is one dimension of a more complex community situation that involves multiple systems.

## Summary: Principles

This skill rests on these commitments:

1. **Land is never neutral.** Every piece of land has a history — Indigenous, political, ecological — and a community that ignores that history is not actually doing stewardship. It is doing occupation without consciousness.

2. **Land tenure is the foundation.** The difference between owning land, renting it, and having informal access determines the community's stability, planning horizon, and capacity for ecological investment. Know what you have and what it means.

3. **Indigenous land requires material engagement, not just acknowledgment.** Land acknowledgment is a starting point. What the community does with that acknowledgment — what relationships it builds, what obligations it meets, what political struggles it supports — is the actual work.

4. **Food production labor is community labor.** Growing, harvesting, cooking, and preserving are not naturally distributed equitably. They require the same explicit mapping, recognition, and governance that any other community labor requires.

5. **Ecological decisions are governance decisions.** How the community treats its soil, water, pest populations, and biodiversity expresses values and requires collective accountability, not just technical expertise.

6. **Animals have lives and deaths that deserve governance.** The ethics of raising and killing animals, the labor of daily care, and the grief of losing community animals are not peripheral concerns. They deserve explicit community conversation and planning.

7. **Place attachment is real.** The embodied, emotional, and spiritual relationship to specific land is not sentiment to be overcome. It is part of what makes land-based community life meaningful, and its loss is genuine loss that deserves genuine grief.

8. **Seasonal rhythms are not background.** The agricultural calendar shapes what is possible when. Communities that honor seasonal rhythms in their governance and expectations live more harmoniously with the land and with each other.

9. **Environmental justice is part of land stewardship.** Who bears environmental burdens, whose land is polluted, how race shapes land access — these are not separate from ecological practice. They are part of the political economy of land that any honest steward must reckon with.

10. **Land-based community life is beautiful and political simultaneously.** The beauty of working soil, watching things grow, eating food you have raised, marking the seasons together is real. So is the politics of whose land you're on, who does the labor, and what the land has meant to others before you. Hold both without pretending either cancels the other out.
