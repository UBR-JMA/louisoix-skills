# Sex-Positivity: Community Advisory Framework

Sex-positivity in a community context is not about promoting any particular form of sexuality or relationship structure. It is about creating conditions where community members can bring their whole selves — including their relational and sexual lives — without shame, judgment, or erasure, while the community maintains the safety, clarity, and relational health it needs to function.

As a steward, your role is not to normalize any particular relationship style or behavior. It is to hold the community with enough wisdom and steadiness that members don't have to hide the parts of themselves that don't fit a particular norm, and that when sexuality or relationship dynamics create friction, you can address them skillfully.

---

## Foundational Framework: What Sex-Positivity Is (and Is Not)

**Sex-positivity is:**
- The assumption that consensual adult sexuality is not inherently shameful, wrong, or a problem to be managed
- Recognition that people have diverse relationship structures, desires, and identities, all of which deserve dignity
- The practice of not adding shame to something that's already hard to talk about
- Creating community norms built on consent and care rather than implicit conformity

**Sex-positivity is NOT:**
- Requiring anyone to discuss their sexuality or relationships with the community
- Treating all sexual choices as equally wise or consequence-free
- Removing the community's right to set reasonable shared norms
- Bypassing accountability when harm has occurred (that's safety-planning territory)
- Removing privacy and discretion as values

**The core question for a steward:** Is the discomfort in this situation coming from a community member's sexuality or relationship structure being *different* — or from it causing *actual harm*? These require entirely different responses. Difference requires hospitality. Harm requires accountability.

## Relationship Diversity in Community Life

Many communities of care include members with non-monogamous or non-traditional relationship structures. This is not new, but it has become more visible. A steward needs to understand these structures not to police them but to support members navigating them well.

### Common Structures and Their Community Dynamics

**Polyamory / ethical non-monogamy (ENM):** Multiple consensual romantic or sexual relationships. The challenges this creates in community are less about the structure and more about: relationship drama affecting community atmosphere, NRE (new relationship energy — the intense early period of a new relationship) pulling members into preoccupation, breakups that leave awkward community geometry, or jealousy/conflict spilling into shared spaces.

**Relationship anarchy:** No hierarchy among relationships; all connections are valued on their own terms. People practicing this may not categorize their relationships in ways others expect. This sometimes creates confusion for community members who assume they know "who is with whom."

**Open relationships:** A primary partnership that allows additional sexual or romantic connections with negotiated agreements. The community dynamics depend heavily on whether agreements are healthy or whether the structure is being used to avoid addressing problems in the primary relationship.

**Queerplatonic partnerships:** Deep, committed non-romantic partnerships that carry the weight of a partnership without romantic or sexual components. Often invisible to community members who don't know this framework exists.

**Solo polyamory:** Maintaining autonomy and independence as a baseline; not seeking a "nesting partner." May look like someone who seems to have many casual connections without committing — which can be misread as avoidance of intimacy rather than a deliberate and healthy choice.

**What a steward should know about all of these:** The presence of these structures isn't the problem. What creates community friction is:
- Poor agreements within the relationship(s)
- NRE that causes someone to become absent from community responsibilities
- Breakups that involve community members on multiple sides
- One relationship style being implicitly or explicitly treated as superior
- Community members treating non-monogamy as license to comment on or inquire into someone's relationship details

## Navigating Jealousy and NRE as Community Dynamics

**New Relationship Energy (NRE)** is the intense, often consuming feeling of early romantic connection. It is real, normal, and often temporary. When community members are in NRE, they may miss commitments, seem less present, over-invest in a new connection at the expense of established ones, and be temporarily less regulated (more reactive, more idealistic, less grounded).

**What a steward can do:** Name it without judgment. "It's pretty visible that you're in an exciting new connection right now. I'm not asking you to dial it back — that's not my business. But I want to check in about [missed commitment] and make sure community responsibilities are covered." NRE isn't a character flaw; it's a state. Naming it directly and practically is kinder than letting resentment build quietly.

**Jealousy in community:** Whether people are in monogamous or non-monogamous relationships, jealousy sometimes shows up in community dynamics — when a member feels displaced by a new relationship, when a friendship is perceived to have been "taken" by a new partner, or when someone is managing active jealousy as part of their relationship structure.

**What helps:** Make space for members to have complex feelings without requiring those feelings to be resolved quickly. Jealousy is not, by itself, a community problem. It becomes one when it drives behavior — triangulation, coalition-building, sniping — that affects others. Address the behavior, not the feeling.

## Sexual Shame and Discovery in Community

Communities of care often include members who are discovering, rediscovering, or finally naming something about their sexuality or relational nature for the first time. This may include:

- Late-discovery of bisexuality, queerness, or non-monogamous orientation
- Discovering after years that one is asexual or aromantic
- A deeply religious person navigating faith and sexuality in tension
- Someone healing from sexual shame after a conservative upbringing
- A person who experienced sexual trauma and is working toward integration

**What these members need from the community:**
- Absence of pressure to explain, justify, or perform their identity
- Not being the subject of gossip or speculation
- Space to be in process without being required to have it figured out
- Genuine welcome for wherever they land, including places that surprise others

**What a steward does:**
- Creates a culture where difference doesn't become gossip
- Checks in privately, without prying: "I've noticed you seem like you're carrying something. I'm not asking you to share anything — I just want you to know I'm here if that would help."
- Intervenes if they hear gossiping about a member's sexuality or relationships: "I'd ask us not to speculate about other members' private lives."
- Holds the longer view: a member who is in a difficult or confusing place right now may need months or years to integrate, not a quick resolution

## Community Norms Around Bodies and Intimacy

Communities of care often involve physical affection — hugging, touch, shared physical spaces — and sometimes have varying members with very different comfort levels. These differences deserve explicit attention rather than implicit assumptions.

**Consent culture as community norm:**
The most useful frame is not rules about specific behaviors but a culture of explicit, ongoing, cheerful consent. Members should be able to say "I'm not a hugger" or "I need some space right now" without it being awkward. Leaders model this by asking: "Can I give you a hug?" and accepting no gracefully.

**Setting shared norms:**
When physical affection or intimacy is creating friction (members who feel uncomfortable, members who feel excluded from affection they'd welcome), surface it directly rather than waiting for it to calcify. "I want to talk about how we handle physical affection in this community as a group. I've heard different things from different people and I think we could do better at naming what we're all comfortable with."

**Physical spaces:**
If the community shares living or gathering spaces, norms about sexual behavior in shared spaces should be explicit, not assumed. This doesn't require being restrictive — it requires being clear. Members have different assumptions and no one should have to discover norms by violation.

## Sexuality, Aging, and Disability

Sexual identity, desire, and relational needs don't end with aging or disability. Community stewards are often in a position to support members whose sexual lives are changing because of age or health.

**What this looks like in community:**
- An older member whose partner died and who is navigating desire and grief together
- A member with a disability whose sexuality has been affected and who experiences shame about this
- A community member whose partner has dementia and who is navigating loneliness and desire in a morally complex situation
- Younger members making assumptions that older or disabled members are asexual

**What a steward holds:** Sexuality doesn't "end." Treating a member's sexuality or relational desires as appropriate only if they're young, able-bodied, and uncomplicated is ageism and ableism. Support includes not making those assumptions, and creating space for members to talk about this without embarrassment.

## Kink-Aware Community Dynamics

Some community members may practice BDSM or kink. This is not inherently a community issue — adult consensual private practice is not the community's business. It can become relevant when:

- A member is experiencing shame about their desires and it's affecting their community functioning
- A member discloses something to the steward seeking support
- Community drama has emerged from a kink relationship that involved community members
- Power dynamics in a kink relationship are being confused with or affecting community power dynamics

**Being kink-aware means:**
- Not treating the disclosure of kink as shocking or as something to manage or fix
- Understanding that consent and care are foundational in healthy kink practice
- Knowing the difference between consensual power exchange and abuse (the latter has clear markers: non-consent, coercion, isolation, harm without negotiation)
- Not requiring members to justify or explain their desires to be supported

**A steward's role** is not to have opinions about what consenting adults do in their private lives. It is to create a community where shame doesn't drive people to hide, and where, when a member needs support around their sexual self, they can get it without judgment.

## When Sexuality Intersects With Harm

Sex-positivity and safety are not in tension. Consent is foundational to sex-positivity — which means that when consent is absent or violated, you are no longer in sex-positivity territory. You are in safety-planning territory.

**Activate safety-planning (not this skill) when:**
- A member reports sexual assault or coercion by another community member
- Pressure or manipulation is being used in sexual or relational contexts
- A member is in a relationship where consent is being systematically overridden
- There are concerns about power imbalances being exploited (mentor/student, steward/new member, therapist/client)

**Sex-positivity supports safety by:**
- Creating a shame-free culture where people can disclose without fear
- Making it easier to name violations because the community doesn't treat sexuality as inherently dirty or shameful
- Modeling consent culture in all community interactions, not just explicitly sexual ones

## Navigating Disagreement in Community About Relationship Structures

Not all community members will hold the same values about relationships and sexuality. This is real and navigable.

**Distinguish private values from community norms:**
A member can personally believe monogamy is the right structure for everyone while still living in a community that includes non-monogamous members without conflict. The question is not whether everyone shares the same values — it is whether the community can hold difference without one set of values becoming the implicit norm that others are measured against.

**When a member's values lead to harmful behavior:**
Expressing a personal view privately is different from creating a hostile environment. If a member is making comments that shame or exclude members for their relationship structures, that's a community culture problem — address the behavior, not the belief.

**The steward's position:**
You don't have to endorse any particular relationship structure. You do have to hold the community in a way that allows members with different structures to be full members. The steward's personal views about relationships are not the community's values.

## Reading a Situation: When Sex-Positivity Is the Active Framework

Activate this skill when:
- A member's relationship structure (or its changes or complications) is creating community dynamics
- A member is navigating sexual shame or identity that's affecting their community engagement
- The community's implicit norms around bodies, affection, or relationships are creating friction
- NRE, jealousy, or relationship drama is affecting community functioning
- A member discloses something about their sexuality or desires and needs a place to land
- Community members are gossiping or speculating about a member's private relational or sexual life
- Physical affection or shared space norms need examination

Stay in this skill (rather than shifting to safety-planning) as long as the issue is about difference, shame, or navigating complexity — not about harm, coercion, or consent violations.

*For consent violations and sexual safety: invoke safety-planning.*
*For LGBTQ+ identity, coming out, and affirmation: invoke lgbtq-affirmation. (Sex-positivity and lgbtq-affirmation often work together — activate both when relevant.)*
*For aging and end-of-life contexts where sexuality intersects: invoke elder-care.*
*For trauma integration when sexual history is shaping someone's experience: invoke trauma-informed-care.*
